Every year, tens of thousands of American pet owners choose to have their dogs treated at accredited international veterinary clinics. In 2025 alone, an estimated **36,670 dogs crossed from the U.S. into Mexico** for veterinary care — an 11.6% increase from the previous year. The Florida-Caribbean corridor is the fastest-growing route.

This guide covers everything you need to know about cross-border veterinary care if you're a Florida pet owner considering this option.

## What Is Cross-Border Veterinary Care?

Cross-border veterinary care means taking your pet to an accredited veterinary clinic outside the United States for a surgical or medical procedure. It's the same model as human medical tourism — accessing the same quality of care at a fraction of the domestic price.

The concept has been validated at scale by companies like MexiVet Express, which now handles approximately **80 pets per week** across the San Diego-Tijuana border with a full-time staff of 15 and 7 dedicated drivers.

---

## Why Is It So Much Cheaper?

This isn't about lower quality — it's about different economics:

- **Facility costs:** International clinic overhead is 50-70% lower
- **Staffing costs:** Veterinary compensation reflects local economies
- **Equipment costs:** The same surgical equipment (digital X-ray, orthopedic implants, anesthesia monitors) costs the same worldwide, but the markup is lower
- **Regulatory costs:** Lower malpractice insurance and compliance costs

The result: **board-certified surgeons using the same techniques and equipment, at 50-80% less cost.**

## Is It Legal? (Yes, With Some Requirements)

Absolutely. The USDA APHIS (Animal and Plant Health Inspection Service) has clear regulations for pet re-entry to the United States. The requirements vary by destination:

### Bahamas (Freeport) — Simplest Option
- USDA health certificate (no endorsement required)
- Rabies vaccination certificate
- Click2Clear import permit ($11.20)
- **No screwworm certificate required**
- **No CDC rabies high-risk designation**

### Mexico (Cancun) — Moderate Requirements
- USDA-endorsed health certificate
- Rabies vaccination certificate
- Screwworm certificate within 5 days of return
- Group charter travel

## Common Misconceptions

**"The quality won't be as good."**
The same surgical techniques, the same implant manufacturers, the same anesthesia protocols are used worldwide. Many international clinics have surgeons trained at U.S. and European veterinary schools.

**"It's risky to travel after surgery."**
We coordinate with the surgeon on the optimal recovery window. For complex procedures, we arrange multi-night stays in managed recovery villas with 24/7 monitoring.

**"My U.S. vet won't be involved."**
Your U.S. primary veterinarian is a critical partner. They provide the initial diagnosis and health certificate, and they handle all post-operative follow-up. We send them a complete surgical report.

## Who Is a Candidate?

Most dogs in good general health are candidates for cross-border care. The best candidates are:

- Dogs needing a procedure costing $3,000+ in the U.S.
- Dogs with a confirmed diagnosis from a U.S. veterinarian
- Dogs current on vaccinations and in good overall health
- Owners who are comfortable with a day-trip or short-stay model

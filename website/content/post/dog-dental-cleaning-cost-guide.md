Dental disease affects **80% of dogs over age 3**, making professional dental cleaning one of the most commonly recommended — and most commonly delayed — veterinary procedures. If you've been putting it off because of the cost, you're not alone.

---

## What Determines the Cost?

The price of a professional dog dental cleaning varies based on several factors:

### 1. Anesthesia (The Biggest Cost Driver)

Most of the cost isn't the cleaning itself — it's the anesthesia and monitoring. Unlike human dental cleanings, dogs require general anesthesia for a safe, thorough cleaning.

| Component | Typical Cost |
|-----------|-------------|
| Pre-anesthetic bloodwork | $75 - $200 |
| Anesthesia induction | $50 - $100 |
| IV catheter + fluids | $50 - $100 |
| Anesthesia monitoring (pulse ox, ECG, BP) | $100 - $250 |

**Total anesthesia-related:** $275 - $650

### 2. The Dental Procedure Itself

| Component | Typical Cost |
|-----------|-------------|
| Dental cleaning & polishing | $150 - $400 |
| Full-mouth digital X-rays | $100 - $300 |
| Per tooth extraction (simple) | $25 - $75 |
| Per tooth extraction (surgical) | $75 - $200 |

### 3. Additional Costs

| Item | Cost |
|------|------|
| Post-op pain medication | $30 - $75 |
| Antibiotics (if infection present) | $20 - $60 |
| Oravet sealant (optional) | $50 - $100 |

## Anesthesia-Free Cleaning: Why It's Not Recommended

You may see mobile "anesthesia-free" dental cleaning services for $100-$200. **Veterinary dental specialists strongly advise against this.** Why:

- You can't properly clean below the gumline without anesthesia — which is where most disease lives
- You can't take dental X-rays — missing hidden disease, infections, and abscesses
- The procedure causes significant stress to the dog
- It gives a false sense of security — teeth look clean on the surface while disease progresses

## Signs Your Dog Needs a Dental Cleaning

- Bad breath (halitosis) — the most common sign
- Yellow or brown tartar buildup on teeth
- Red, inflamed gums (gingivitis)
- Bleeding when chewing or eating
- Pawing at the mouth
- Dropping food while eating
- Reluctance to eat hard food or chew toys

## How Often Does Your Dog Need a Cleaning?

| Risk Level | Frequency |
|-----------|-----------|
| Small breeds (Chihuahua, Yorkie, Maltese) | Every 6-12 months |
| Medium breeds (Beagle, Corgi, French Bulldog) | Every 12-18 months |
| Large breeds (Labrador, Golden Retriever, GSD) | Every 18-24 months |
| Dogs with good home care (brushing, chews) | Every 18-36 months |

---

## At-Home Care Between Cleanings

- **Daily brushing** with enzymatic toothpaste is the gold standard
- **Dental chews** (VOHC-approved, like Greenies or CET chews)
- **Dental diet** (Prescription Diet t/d, Hill's Dental Care)
- **Water additives** — less effective than brushing but better than nothing
- **Dental wipes** for dogs who won't tolerate a brush

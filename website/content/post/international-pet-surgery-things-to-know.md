Making the decision to pursue international veterinary care for your dog is a big step. The savings are real — 50-80% on most procedures — but there are practical considerations that every pet owner should understand before traveling.

Here are the 10 most important things to know.

---

### 1. The Health Certificate Has a Window

Your dog needs a **USDA-accredited health certificate** issued within **10 days of travel**. This means you can't get it too early, but you also can't arrive at the terminal without it. For our Freeport day-trips, the certificate does not require USDA endorsement (a significant time and cost saver). For Cancun trips, USDA endorsement is required.

**Tip:** Schedule your health certificate appointment for 7-10 days before your trip date.

### 2. Fasting Is Non-Negotiable

Your dog must be **fasted (no food) after midnight** on the day of surgery. Water is usually okay until 6 AM. This is a standard safety requirement for general anesthesia — a dog with food in its stomach is at higher risk for aspiration during surgery.

### 3. The First 24 Hours Are Critical

After anesthesia, your dog will be groggy, disoriented, and may whine or seem confused. This is normal. Keep them in a quiet, confined space, offer small amounts of water, and don't panic if they don't want to eat right away.

**What's NOT normal:** Pale gums, difficulty breathing, continuous bleeding, inability to stand, or severe vomiting. Call us immediately if you see these.

### 4. The E-Collar Is Mandatory

Your dog will come home with an Elizabethan collar (e-collar). It must be worn **at all times** for 10-14 days, except during supervised feeding. Dogs can reopen an incision in seconds by licking or chewing. Don't be tempted to remove it because your dog looks uncomfortable — that discomfort is temporary; a surgical site infection is not.

### 5. Your U.S. Vet Is Still Your Primary Care Provider

International surgery is not a replacement for your relationship with your regular veterinarian. They provide the initial diagnosis, the health certificate, and all post-operative follow-up care. We send them a complete surgical report within 24 hours, and the operating surgeon is available for a direct handoff call.

### 6. Activity Restrictions Are Strict

For most surgeries, your dog is restricted to **leash walks only for bathroom breaks** for 7-14 days. No running, jumping, stair climbing, dog parks, or rough play. For orthopedic procedures (TPLO, fracture repair), confinement lasts 8-12 weeks.

Ignoring activity restrictions is the #1 cause of surgical complications.

### 7. Medications Must Be Given Exactly as Prescribed

You'll receive discharge medications with a dosing schedule. Give them exactly as directed — don't skip doses, don't stop early because your dog "seems fine," and **never give over-the-counter human pain relievers** (ibuprofen, acetaminophen, naproxen), which are toxic to dogs.

### 8. The Follow-Up Appointment Is Not Optional

Your dog needs a post-operative recheck with your primary U.S. veterinarian within **14 days** of the procedure. This is when the vet checks the incision, removes non-absorbable sutures or staples, and ensures healing is on track. We pay your vet a follow-up fee for this examination.

### 9. Bathing and Swimming Are Off-Limits

The incision must stay **completely dry** for 10-14 days. No baths, no swimming, no rain walks without a protective covering. If the incision gets wet, pat it dry immediately and check for signs of infection (redness, discharge, odor).

You're not alone in this. Your MedPup concierge is available throughout the trip and during recovery. Our 24-hour emergency line connects you with someone who can help, day or night. And your U.S. veterinarian is always your first call for any medical concerns.

## Questions to Ask Before Booking

Before you schedule a MedPup trip, make sure you have clear answers to these questions:

1. Does my dog have a confirmed diagnosis from my U.S. vet?
2. Is my dog in good enough general health for anesthesia and travel?
3. What is the exact U.S. quote I've received? (We'll compare this to our pricing)
4. Do I have a preferred travel date in mind?
5. Does my dog have a current rabies vaccination?
6. Do I have a regular veterinarian who can provide the health certificate and follow-up care?

## What Affects the Cost of Mass Removal

### Size and Complexity
- **Simple masses** (small, superficial, easily accessible): $500-$1,500
- **Complex masses** (large, deep, near vital structures): $1,500-$3,000

### Location
- **Trunk/legs:** Lower cost — straightforward access
- **Head/face:** Higher cost — more complex closure, cosmetic considerations
- **Mouth:** Highest cost — requires dental x-rays, specialist involvement

### Histopathology (Biopsy)
Every removed mass should be sent to a lab for analysis. This determines whether the lump is benign or malignant and guides any follow-up treatment needed.

| Analysis Type | Cost | Results Time |
|---------------|------|-------------|
| Cytology (needle sample before removal) | $50 - $150 | Same day |
| Histopathology (full biopsy after removal) | $200 - $500 | 7-10 days |
| Immunohistochemistry (specialized cancer marker) | $200 - $500 | 10-14 days |

## Common Types of Skin Masses

### Benign (usually harmless)
| Type | Description | Recurrence Risk |
|------|------------|----------------|
| Lipoma | Fatty tumor, common in older dogs | Low if completely removed |
| Sebaceous adenoma | Overgrowth of oil glands | Low |
| Histiocytoma | Benign skin growth, common in young dogs | May resolve on its own |
| Papilloma | Wart-like, viral | May resolve on its own |
| Cyst | Fluid-filled sac | Low if sac completely removed |

### Malignant (require aggressive treatment)
| Type | Description | Treatment |
|------|------------|-----------|
| Mast cell tumor | Most common skin cancer in dogs | Surgical removal + clean margins |
| Soft tissue sarcoma | Connective tissue cancer | Wide surgical excision |
| Melanoma | Pigmented skin cancer | Surgical removal +/- immunotherapy |
| Squamous cell carcinoma | Skin/connective tissue | Surgical removal |

**Important:** You cannot tell if a mass is benign or malignant by looking at it. Even if the lump looks harmless, histopathology is essential.

## What to Do If You Find a Lump

1. **Don't panic.** Most skin masses in dogs are benign.
2. **Monitor for changes.** Note size, shape, color, and growth rate.
3. **See your vet.** They'll perform a fine needle aspirate (cytology) for initial assessment.
4. **Discuss removal.** Even benign masses can cause problems if they grow large enough.

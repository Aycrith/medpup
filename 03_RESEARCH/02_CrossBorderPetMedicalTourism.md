## 2.1 THE TREND: Quantified Growth

Pet medical tourism is no longer anecdotal — it is a documented, rapidly growing industry.

### Mexico Border Crossing Data (Mexican Government Statistics)

| Year | Dogs Entering Mexico from Abroad | Year-over-Year Change |
|------|----------------------------------|----------------------|
| 2019 | 18,533 | Pre-pandemic baseline |
| 2020 | 14,257 | -23.1% (pandemic border closures) |
| 2023 | 32,869 | — |
| 2024 | **36,670** | **+11.6%** |

**Source:** Data provided to VIN News by the Mexican government. Growth has been "steadily increasing each year since 2020"[reference:26].

**Key takeaway:** 36,670 dogs entered Mexico from abroad in 2024. If even 30% were for veterinary purposes (conservative), that's approximately 11,000 medical tourism cases — and growing at double-digit rates.

### Media Coverage Escalation

The trend has attracted significant mainstream media attention:

- **ABC News / Nightline** (June 2024): Featured MexiVet Express and the growing pet medical tourism industry, interviewing founder Anna Ginsky[reference:27].
- **VIN News / Veterinary Partner** (March 2025): Major industry publication covered "Pet owners turn to veterinary tourism as costs rise at home" — a front-page feature[reference:28].
- **FareVet** (July 2025): "Why Americans Are Crossing the Border for Vet Care" — analyzed the systemic failures driving the trend[reference:29].
- **Audacy** (April 2026): Reported spending on pets expected to increase 7-10% by end of 2025, far outpacing general inflation[reference:30].

## 2.3 MEXICO COST STRUCTURE: Why Prices Are Lower

Ronald Richards of Vet Playas Clinic explains the price differential candidly:

> "The doctors just don't make as much money as they do in the United States. And then you have the cost of [lower overhead]..."[reference:37]

Key factors:
1. **Labor costs:** Mexican veterinary specialists earn significantly less than U.S. counterparts.
2. **Facility overhead:** Commercial real estate, malpractice insurance, and regulatory compliance costs are lower.
3. **Equipment parity:** Top-tier Mexican clinics invest in the same digital X-ray, ultrasound, and surgical equipment as U.S. clinics.
4. **Targeted marketing:** Mexican vets are "making announcements in English" and "advertising more on the internet" to attract U.S. clients[reference:38].

## 2.4 BAHAMAS SPECIFIC LANDSCAPE

The Bahamas market is less developed for veterinary tourism than Mexico, creating a **first-mover advantage** for MedPup.

### Existing Veterinary Infrastructure in Freeport

- **Freeport Veterinary Hospital**: Located in Freeport, Grand Bahama, situated close to the Bahamas Faith Ministries church and the Iglesia Ni Cristo community center[reference:39].
- **Purrfect Pets (Nassau)**: BVMA-accredited clinic offering preventative, medical, surgical, dental, and hospitalized care[reference:40].
- **Animal Medical Center**: Full-service small and large animal care including vaccinations, spay/neuter, ultrasound, cold laser, surgical laser, digital X-ray, endoscopy, and regenerative medicine[reference:41].

### Key Insight
The Bahamas already has accredited veterinary clinics with surgical capabilities. The gap is not in clinical infrastructure — it is in **coordination services** (travel logistics, USDA paperwork, client accompaniment). MedPup fills this gap.

## 2.5 REGULATORY NOTE: Screwworm Requirements (Mexico)

As of November 22, 2024, Mexico is considered affected with screwworm. All dogs returning to the U.S. from Mexico must meet APHIS requirements for screwworm freedom certification upon re-entry[reference:42]. This adds a procedural layer that MedPup's concierge model handles (per the Screwworm Compliance SOP in Phase 1). Bahamas day-trips are not subject to this requirement, making Freeport the lower-friction entry point.

## 2.6 KEY INSIGHT FOR MEDPUP POSITIONING

The cross-border pet medical tourism market is:
1. **Proven** — 36,670+ dogs entering Mexico annually, with double-digit growth.
2. **Media-validated** — ABC, VIN News, and mainstream outlets cover it regularly.
3. **Geographically concentrated** — the dominant corridor is California-Tijuana. The Florida-Bahamas/Cancun corridor is underserved.
4. **Emotionally driven** — clients are not bargain hunters; they are "survivalists" who "refuse to give up" on their pets[reference:43].
5. **Underserved in the East Coast** — Florida's 59.8 million U.S. dog-owning households, concentrated in the Southeast, currently have no dedicated concierge service bridging to the Bahamas or Cancun.

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY: IS THIS A VIABLE BUSINESS?

**Short answer: The demand is real and massive. The supply side is completely unserved in exactly one niche — Florida-based concierge for cross-border procedures. But the "Freeport" path is not yet verified as operationalizable. The Cancún path is better verified today.**

The US pet medical tourism market for *procedures* (not just relocation) is essentially in its infancy. The travel infrastructure (pet relocation companies: AirAnimal, PetRelocation) exists but is focused on moving pets, not coordinating veterinary care. The *medical* side — booking vet appointments, managing post-op recovery, handling health certificates, insurance coordination — is unserved by anyone specific to Florida→Caribbean/Latin America.

**Market demand is not in question:**
- $2.4 billion global pet travel services market (GM Insights 2025), growing 8.3% CAGR to $5.3B by 2034
- 78% of US pet owners travel with their pets annually (Dogster 2026)
- 95 million US households own a pet; 71.6% of all US households (APPA/World Animal Foundation 2026)
- US pet industry spending: $158 billion in 2024 (APPA)
- Vet costs up 47% since 2020 (BLS)
- 54% of pet owners plan to travel with their pet; 58% would prefer to travel with their pet over a friend/family member (Dogster)
- Cancún received **20.1 million international passengers in 2024** alone (The Cancun Sun, confirmed via airport authority)
- **36,670+ dogs/year** crossed into Mexico for veterinary care in 2025 (Mexican government data)
- MexiVet Express operates ~80 pets/week at San Diego-Tijuana — annualizing to ~4,100/year at one border point

**Supply gap:** No single operator provides end-to-end concierge for Florida pet owners traveling abroad for veterinary procedures. AirAnimal and PetRelocation move pets. MexiVet Express is land-border only (San Diego). VetCancun/CAP Cancún are destination clinics — they don't coordinate travel from Florida. **The gap is: "Florida concierge → international vet → return trip coordination."**

## 6. BUSINESS MODEL VARIANTS

### 6.1 Model A — Day-Surgery Concierge (Original MedPup)

Florida → [destination] → procedure → same-day return

**Best for:** Preventive procedures (dental cleaning, mass removal, TPLO same-day protocols)
**Requirements:** Destination with confirmed accept-same-day-international clinic; compliant import/return regime
**Profitability:** 2–3 pets per trip at $900 concierge fee; ~20–35% margin in best cases

### 6.2 Model B — Recovery Week (Extended Stay)

Florida → [destination] → procedure + 3–7 day recovery → return

**Best for:** Complex orthopedic (TPLO/FHO where same-day return not recommended), dental extractions with multi-day anesthesia recovery
**Requirements:** Destination with post-op recovery accommodation; potentially destination-side boarding/kennel partnerships
**Premium pricing:** $1,500–$3,000/concierge for orthopedic; insurance reimbursement potential

### 6.3 Model C — Wellness Tourism (Spaying/Neutering)

Florida → [destination] → sterilization + vacation package → return

**Best for:** Low-income Florida pet owners who can't afford US spay/neuter; high-volume, mass-market
**Destination advantage:** HOPE-level pricing ($75–$450); Cuba if restrictions lifted; Costa Rica/Panamá
**Pricing:** $400–$800 concierge inclusive (undercuts US by significant margin)

### 6.4 Model D — Mobile Surgical Pop-up

US vet → travels to destination with surgical equipment → performs procedures at destination clinic → no permanent destination infrastructure required

**Best for:** Can be applied to any destination; eliminates destination-partner dependency
**Requirements:** Portable surgical equipment, US board-certified surgeon willing to travel, destination veterinary space rental, customs clearance for medical equipment
**Risk:** US→destination medical equipment import, malpractice liability across jurisdictions, sedation/pain management protocol variation

### 6.5 Model E — Hybrid Platform (Digital Concierge)

Digital platform that coordinates all documentation, books destination clinic, manages insurance paperwork, without physically guaranteeing the pet's arrival — client books their own flight + pick-up + recovery

**Best for:** Low capital, operates similarly to FareVet but for medical tourism procedures
**Requirements:** Verified clinic partnerships, document generation software, insurance integration
**Revenue:** Transaction fee per case ($200–$500), potentially insurance referral fees

## 7. COMPETITIVE POSITION

### 7.1 What is the Differentiable Market?

The **Florida-based** angle is the differentiator. Florida has:
- Largest retiree/pet-owner population in the US (3.3M+ resident dogs/cats; APPA-based)
- Most international airport connections in the US for Caribbean/Latin American travel (MIA, FLL, TPA, PBI)
- Specific US regulatory edge: APHIS endorsement handling, CITES documentation, rabies history knowledge

A **Florida**-headquartered concierge for Caribbean/Latin American vet care has a structural advantage no other US geography can match for proximity + connectivity to the Bahamas/Caribbean/Central America.

**Cancún is the market where you'll face competition from in-destination operators who could try to absorb the Florida market themselves (by opening a Florida-facing phone number, running ads in Miami).** Aruba/Turks & Caicos/PR are zero-competition entry markets.

### 7.3 Puerto Rico — The Sleeped-On Candidate

Puerto Rico has:
- Zero import complexity (just like traveling from New York to Florida by air)
- A 7,000 sq ft specialist referral hospital 2.5h from FLL/MIA (CEVET)
- Surgery listed; radiology, internal medicine, ophthalmology confirmed
- No existing pet medical tourism operator
- English widely spoken (US territory)
- No screwworm; no screwworm certificate; no APHIS endorsement; no import permit; no CDC high-risk; no visa

**The only reason Puerto Rico isn't the obvious answer is that the procedures might cost more than Cancún.** PR vet prices are likely US-price-adjacent (it's a US economy with US regulation and cost structure) — dentistry is charged at US rates or slightly below. If US dentistry is $500–$3,000+ per procedure, PR dentistry might be $400–$2,500. The margin compression could be severe. **This is a critical unknown that requires price inquiry.**

**Cancún** has possibly the lowest procedure costs in the region but requires screwworm certificate, SENASICA permit, APHIS endorsement, import process — all friction that Puerto Rico eliminates.

**Freeport** has zero import friction (BAHFSA $50, 24–48h) but zero confirmed surgical capability. The ferry is a unique advantage but treated as a secondary point since direct flights would also be available.

## 8. DEMAND SIDE: ACTUAL OPPORTUNITY SIZE

### 8.1 Florida Pet Owner Base

- Florida: ~8 million households; ~21 million people
- Using 71.6% US household pet ownership rate → ~5.7 million Florida pet-owning households
- If 10% of those own a dog and 78% travel annually → ~444,000 dog-owning Florida households that travel with their pet annually

### 8.2 TAM for Pet Medical Tourism from Florida

- Total US pet medical tourism estimates vary (industry not well quantified)
- Cancún airport: 20.1 million international arrivals in 2024 (single airport)
- Even **0.01%** of Cancún's US visitors traveling with a pet that needs veterinary care = 2,010 potential cases/year from Cancún alone
- Florida accounts for ~15-20% of US outbound travel to Cancún
- Lower bound: **300–500 Florida pets/year** seeking care in Cancún (conservative, not capturing all US pets traveling from Florida to all destinations)

### 8.3 Penetration Potential

- If a MedPup concierge captures **10 pet cases per trip × 20 trips per year = 200 pets/year** in Cancún
- $900 × 200 = $180,000 revenue/year (purely concierge fees, before margin)
- Meanwhile: 36,670 pets/year crossed at San Diego-Tijuana for vet care — MexiVet Express alone at ~4,000/year
- **Market is being validated by existing border operations** — question is whether air-based tourists will also travel for care

### 8.4 Barriers to Demand Realization

- **Pet owner awareness** of this option is near-zero today
- **Trust**: owners won't send pets abroad unless process feels safe and legitimate
- **Insurance reimbursement**: unclear whether pet insurance covers cross-border procedures
- **Logistics fear**: the documentation layer is intimidating (health cert, import permit, APHIS endorsement, screwworm)
- **Recovery concern**: post-op recovery in a hotel room vs. US vet hospital
- → All of these are solved by the concierge proposition. The value is eliminating the buyer's friction.

## 11. COMPETITIVE POSITIONING: PET RELOCATION vs. PET MEDICAL TOURISM

The pet relocation operators (AirAnimal, PetRelocation, PetAir) are NOT competitors. They do not coordinate veterinary procedures. They move pets, prepare paperwork, and handle transport logistics. A medical tourism concierge handles: **"I need my dog's TPLO done in Cancún in 2 weeks, don't make me figure out health certs, vet clinics, ferries, hotel, and post-op recovery."** That is an entirely different value proposition. Pet relocation operators could become **distribution partners** (consolidating procedures for bulk movement).

MexiVet Express is the closest direct competitor operating a border model (San Diego-Tijuana), but operates:
- Land border only (passenger walks across, not pet in cargo hold)
- Tijuana explicitly (border zone, different from tourist destination)
- Daily repeatable cycles with known routings
**A Florida-based air model is structurally different** and MexiVet Express would need to prove it can manage Florida→Cancún flights, air-cargo research and booking, airport-to-clinic logistics — none of which is confirmed for MexiVet.

### Critical Regulatory Distinction

**Florida's "limited-service veterinary medical practice" definition does NOT cover a permanent surgical center.** Florida Administrative Code defines "limited-service" as "a privately or publicly supported vaccination clinic where a veterinarian performs for a limited time vaccinations and/or immunizations against disease on multiple animals". This means a permanent surgical center would need a **standard premises permit** under Fla. Stat. §474.215, not a limited-service permit.

This is actually advantageous: the standard premises permit has no time or service restrictions. The facility would need to meet minimum standards for sanitary conditions, recordkeeping, equipment, radiation monitoring, and physical plant. There is no requirement to offer full-service care — you can limit your scope voluntarily.

### Addressable Market Calculation

Using Miami-Dade County as the initial target market:

- **975,411 households** in Miami-Dade County.
- Assuming **56-66% pet ownership** = 546,230 to 643,771 pet-owning households.
- Using the more conservative Florida-specific 56% rate: **~546,230 pet-owning households**.
- Applying 71% who cite cost as a barrier: **~387,823 cost-constrained pet-owning households**.
- Narrowing to those within a **10-mile radius** of a centrally located clinic (e.g., near Miami International Airport or along a major corridor like US-441 or Palmetto Expressway) could conservatively capture **25-35% of the county population** = **~97,000-136,000 cost-constrained households**.

This income-to-cost gap suggests a large population of "ALICE" households (Asset Limited, Income Constrained, Employed) — precisely the demographic Dr. Warren identifies as her target clients ($30K-$100K income).

## 9. Answering the Core Question

**Can a low-cost, limited-scope surgical/dental center in South Florida be profitable while charging prices 30-50% below full-service veterinary hospitals?**

### Evidence Assessment

**1. Proven Model Exists:** Dr. Kelly's Surgical Unit has demonstrated that a for-profit, surgery-only model can be highly scalable, growing from 0 to 7+ locations in 10 years with consistent annual growth rates of 50-160%. Focus Veterinary Care shows the model works at the lean end (1 vet, 2 techs).

**2. Pricing Is Verified:** Dr. Kelly's published pricing consistently achieves 50-70% savings versus full-service vets. Emancipet's $225-$425 dental pricing versus $300-$3,000+ at full-service confirms the cost advantage.

**3. Regulatory Path Exists:** Florida allows permanent veterinary premises with a standard permit. No requirement for full-service scope exists. Non-veterinarian ownership is permitted. The "limited-service" designation in Florida is for temporary vaccination clinics, not permanent surgical centers — but this does not block the model; it just means operating under a standard premises permit.

**4. Unit Economics Are Favorable:**
- Lean staffing (1 vet + 2-3 techs) keeps labor costs manageable. At Florida salaries: ~$120K for a vet + ~$130K for 3 techs = ~$250K annual labor.
- Lease costs at $44-46/sq ft for a modest 1,500-2,000 sq ft facility = ~$66,000-$92,000/year.
- Equipment startup costs (anesthesia machine, monitors, X-ray, surgical instruments): ~$30,000-$60,000.
- Total estimated annual fixed costs: ~$350,000-$400,000.
- At 20-25 procedures/day × 5 days/week × 48 weeks = 4,800-6,000 procedures/year, the break-even per procedure is ~$58-$83. This is achievable given procedure prices of $125-$1,200.

**5. Market Demand Is Massive:** ~546,000 pet-owning households in Miami-Dade alone, with ~71% cost-constrained. The median income-to-survival-budget gap creates a large ALICE demographic. Florida ranks 8th nationally in veterinary service search demand.

### Conclusion

**Yes, the model is viable in South Florida.** The combination of (a) a proven business model with verified pricing, (b) a regulatory pathway that allows focused-scope practice, (c) favorable unit economics, and (d) massive unmet demand in a high-pet-ownership, income-constrained market strongly supports the feasibility of a profitable low-cost surgical center in Miami-Dade, Broward, or Palm Beach counties.

The primary risk is the **absence of existing Florida competitors** in this specific niche — which also represents the primary opportunity as a first-mover in an underserved market.
